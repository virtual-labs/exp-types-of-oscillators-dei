<h2>Simple Harmonic Motion</h2>
<p>Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of oscillating motion. A pendulum or an object hanging from a spring will oscillate harmonically when there is a small deviation from its equilibrium or rest state. A restoring force proportionate to <em>x</em> is created when slight displacement <em>x</em> occurs from the equilibrium position. This force always acts in the opposite direction of the displacement, towards the equilibrium position.</p>

<p><strong>F = &minus;kx &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(1)</strong></p>
<p>where <em>k</em> is the spring constant (force constant). This motion is used to model many physical systems in the real world where a mass oscillates about an equilibrium position.</p>

<p>The general equation governing simple harmonic motion is:</p>
<p><strong>m&#x1E8D; + b&#x1E8B; + kx = F<sub>0</sub> sin(&omega;t + &phi;) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(3)</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>m : Mass of the ball in kg</li>
  <li>F<sub>0</sub> : Force in Newton (N)</li>
  <li>b : Damping factor in Ns/m</li>
  <li>k : Spring constant in N/m</li>
  <li>&omega; : Angular frequency in rad/sec</li>
  <li>t : Time in sec</li>
  <li>&phi; : Phase angle</li>
</ul>

<h2><u>Free Oscillators</u></h2>
<p>Free oscillations, such as the swinging motion of a pendulum or the vibration of an elastic rod, occur when the motion is sustained by gravitational or elastic restoring forces. Undamped free vibration is the simplest form of vibrating motion.</p>
<p><strong>Examples:</strong> Simple pendulum oscillations, short-range tuning fork sound, sounds of musical instruments such as organ pipes.</p>

<h3><u>Nature of Free Oscillators</u></h3>
<p>In free oscillations, the amplitude and frequency of a freely vibrating body remain constant. During this motion, the system neither gains nor loses energy.</p>
<p>In this type of motion, both the damping factor (b) and the forcing amplitude (F<sub>0</sub>) are equal to zero. Thus, equation (3) becomes:</p>
<p><strong>m&#x1E8D; + kx = 0 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(4)</strong></p>
<p>The solution of this equation is:</p>
<p><strong>x(t) = A cos(&omega;<sub>0</sub>t + &phi;) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(5)</strong></p>
<p>which represents simple harmonic motion, where &omega;<sub>0</sub> is the natural angular frequency of the system:</p>
<p><strong>&omega;<sub>0</sub> = &radic;(k/m)</strong></p>

<h2><u>Damped Oscillators</u></h2>
<p>When a moving particle interacts with resistive forces like air resistance or friction, it gradually loses kinetic energy in damped oscillations. A particle's displacement gradually decreases over time and eventually reaches its state of rest as a result of the resistance provided by external forces.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> Shock absorbers in vehicles.</p>
<p>If the damping force F<sub>0</sub> is proportional to the velocity v<sub>0</sub> with a damping constant b, then:</p>
<p><strong>F<sub>0</sub> = &minus;bv<sub>0</sub> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(6)</strong></p>
<p>The equation of motion (3) becomes:</p>
<p><strong>m&#x1E8D; + b&#x1E8B; + kx = 0 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(7)</strong></p>
<p>The solution of equation (7) is:</p>
<p><strong>x(t) = Ae<sup>&minus;&gamma;t</sup> cos(&omega;t + &phi;) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(8)</strong></p>
<p>where &gamma; = b/2m, and the amplitude is given by:</p>
<p><strong>A = F<sub>0</sub> / ( m &radic;[ (&omega;<sup>2</sup> &minus; &omega;<sub>0</sub><sup>2</sup>)<sup>2</sup> + 4&gamma;<sup>2</sup>m<sup>2</sup> ] ) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(9)</strong></p>
<p>The frequency of the damped oscillator is:</p>
<p><strong>&omega; = &radic;( k/m &minus; b<sup>2</sup>/4m<sup>2</sup> ) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(10)</strong></p>
<p>Critical condition: b<sup>2</sup> = 4km, i.e., b = 2&radic;2 (for m = 1 kg, k = 2 N/m)</p>

<p><strong>Damping is of three types:</strong></p>

<strong><u><p>1. Under Damping</p></u></strong>
<p>When damping is small, the system vibrates at first approximately as if there were no damping, but the amplitude of the oscillations decreases exponentially.<br>
<strong>Example:</strong> A swinging pendulum decreasing in amplitude until it comes to a stop.<br>
<u>Under damped condition:</u> b &lt; 2&radic;2</p>
<p><strong>x(t) = Ae<sup>&minus;&gamma;t</sup> cos(&omega;t + &phi;) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(11)</strong></p>
<p>where &gamma; = b/2m, b: damping factor, m: mass of the ball in kg.</p>
<p><strong>A = F<sub>0</sub> / ( m &radic;[ (&omega;<sup>2</sup> &minus; &omega;<sub>0</sub><sup>2</sup>)<sup>2</sup> + 4&gamma;<sup>2</sup>m<sup>2</sup> ] ) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(12)</strong></p>

<strong><u><p>2. Critical Damping</p></u></strong>
<p>When a critically damped oscillator is displaced from the equilibrium, it returns to its equilibrium position in the shortest possible time without oscillating. Depending on the initial velocity, the system either moves monotonically towards zero or reaches a maximum displacement before reversing direction.<br>
<strong>Example:</strong> Car suspension systems that prevent the car from oscillating after travelling over a bump.<br>
<u>Critical damping condition:</u> b = 2&radic;2 (&approx; 2.8)</p>

<strong><u><p>3. Over Damping</p></u></strong>
<p>When a heavily damped oscillator is displaced from the equilibrium, it takes a long time to return to its equilibrium position without oscillating. When damping is large, the system does not oscillate at all.<br>
<strong>Example:</strong> Car suspension systems that prevent the car from oscillating after crossing a bump.<br>
<u>Over damped condition:</u> b &gt; 2.8</p>

<h2><u>Forced Oscillators</u></h2>
<p>Forced oscillations occur when an oscillating system is driven by a periodic external force.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> Applying a periodic force on a swinging pendulum; turning a radio dial to a specific frequency.</p>
<p>The related equation is:</p>
<p><strong>m&#x1E8D; + b&#x1E8B; + kx = F<sub>0</sub> sin(&omega;t + &phi;) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(14)</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>&omega; &nbsp;: Angular frequency for forced oscillations (rad/sec)</li>
  <li>F<sub>0</sub> : Force amplitude for the forced oscillator (N)</li>
</ul>

<h3><u>Resonance</u></h3>
<p>If the forcing frequency is close to the natural frequency of the system and the system is lightly damped, huge vibration amplitudes may occur. This phenomenon is known as <strong>resonance</strong>.</p>
<p>Examples of resonance:</p>
<ol>
  <li>Musical instruments (acoustic resonance).</li>
  <li>Most clocks keep time by mechanical resonance in a balance wheel, pendulum, or quartz crystal.</li>
  <li>Tidal resonance.</li>
  <li>Orbital resonance of some moons in the solar system.</li>
  <li>Resonance of the basilar membrane in the ear.</li>
  <li>Increasing the height of a child's swing by pushing it at each swing.</li>
  <li>A wineglass breaking when a loud sound is produced at its resonant frequency.</li>
</ol>

<p>Some other types of oscillators are:</p>
<center><h3><u><strong>Coupled Oscillators</strong></u></h3></center>
<p>Coupled oscillations occur when two or more oscillating systems are connected in such a way that energy can be exchanged between them. Coupled oscillators occur in nature, for example, in the Earth-Moon system, and in biological systems such as natural pacemakers in the heart.</p>
